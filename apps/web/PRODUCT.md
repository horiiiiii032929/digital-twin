# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The primary users are professors, teaching staff, and project reviewers setting up
a Course Digital Twin before it is exposed to students. They are in a focused
review workflow: checking source permissions, confirming tutor policy behavior,
inspecting preview evidence, and clearing release blockers with synthetic or
approved course material.

## Product Purpose

The onboarding surface helps instructors create a draft tutor policy that is
grounded in approved course sources, aligned with the instructor's teaching
style, and held in draft state until the professor has reviewed the release
evidence. Success means the professor can see what is complete, what remains
blocked, why a decision was made, and how to revise the policy before any
student-facing use.

## Brand Personality

Precise, calm, and accountable. The interface should feel like a serious review
console for sensitive teaching material: direct enough for repeated use, quiet
enough to support careful decisions, and transparent about evidence, permissions,
and release gates.

## Anti-references

This should not feel like a generic AI chat demo, a decorative SaaS landing page,
or an opaque automation panel. Avoid oversized hero treatment, ornamental card
grids, purple-blue gradient AI branding, hidden release blockers, and vague
"magic" language around tutor behavior.

## Design Principles

- Lead with release readiness: the next blocking decision should be visible
  before secondary logs or detail panels.
- Make evidence auditable: source labels, preview decisions, policy fields, and
  approval checklist state should connect back to the release gate.
- Keep the professor in control: revisions are proposed, explained, and confirmed
  explicitly before policy changes land.
- Prefer dense clarity over decoration: the surface should support scanning,
  comparison, and repeated review work.
- Protect sensitive data by default: uploaded course files remain metadata-only
  in Sprint 1, and synthetic examples should be the visible norm.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast and keyboard-operable review controls. Motion should be
limited to state feedback and respect reduced-motion preferences. Color should
never be the only indicator of status; release blockers, source permissions, and
approval states need text and icon support. Use clear labels for assistive
technology and avoid relying on private course details in examples.
